The Central Paradox

The problem is never
the intervention.

Organisations invest in the right training. Governments design evidence-based policy. Clinicians follow best-practice protocols. And yet — the system doesn't change.

The assumption behind almost every intervention framework is that failure means the wrong tool was chosen. So we build better tools. More nuanced models. Richer inventories of what's missing.

But what if the problem isn't the tool? What if the problem is when and in what order the tool is applied — and that applying the right tool at the wrong moment in the wrong sequence actively makes things worse?

The CARA Claim
Intervention effectiveness is determined not by content, but by the system's state and trajectory at the moment of application.
03 / 15
What's Missing

Every framework asks what. None ask when.

COM-B tells you what is missing — Capability, Opportunity, Motivation. The Behaviour Change Wheel tells you which intervention category addresses each gap. BJ Fogg's B=MAP tells you the conditions for behaviour to occur.

All three are inventories. They describe a system's components at a point in time. None of them ask: what direction is the system moving? Which constraint is binding right now? What will happen if I add capacity before the system has an accurate model of what to do with it?

The missing layer is diagnosis before prescription — understanding the system's internal configuration, not just its resource levels.

01

Existing frameworks

Identify what's absent. Prescribe what to add. Assume more of the right thing produces improvement.
02

The reality

Adding the right thing in the wrong state, or in the wrong order, produces committed errors — not recovery.
03

What CARA adds

A diagnostic prior. Before reaching for the tool library, identify what state the system is in and what direction it is moving.

The Core Variables

Every adaptive system
has two jobs.

Function I

Context Awareness

The accuracy of the system's internal model of its situation. Not how much information it receives — but how well it understands what is happening and what comes next.

A person's accurate self-model of their behaviour.
An organisation's honest reading of its market position.
A policy system's grounded understanding of ground conditions.

Function A

Response Availability

The degrees of freedom available to act coherently on that model. Not raw resources or effort — but whether the execution environment is open, directed, and aligned.

A person's real environmental opportunity to change.
An organisation's structural capacity to execute strategy.
A policy system's institutional delivery infrastructure.

The Key Insight
Neither function alone determines outcomes. What matters is their alignment — and the direction each is moving.
05 / 15
The Coherence Constraint

Misalignment is
not just inefficiency.
It is a structural penalty.

Most frameworks treat capacity as additive. More capability plus more opportunity equals more behaviour. Double the investment, double the output. The assumption is compensatory — high motivation can substitute for low ability.

This is wrong. When Context Awareness and Response Availability are misaligned, the system pays an exponential penalty on its effective drive — regardless of how high the absolute levels are.

A highly resourced organisation executing against a broken strategic model is not just suboptimal. It is in a qualitatively different and more dangerous state than a modestly resourced organisation with an accurate one. It is running fast in the wrong direction.

The Coherence Principle
High I + High A + Aligned
Peak performance
High I + High A + Misaligned
Severe penalty — worse than low capacity + aligned
Low I + Low A + Aligned
Constrained but coherent — predictable
The gap between I and A, not the level of either, determines effective drive.

The Myth of Abhimanyu

He could enter
the labyrinth.
He could not exit.

In the Mahabharata, the warrior Abhimanyu learned the secret of entering the Chakravyuha — an unbreakable battle formation — while still in his mother's womb. He heard his father Arjuna explain how to enter. But before Arjuna could describe how to exit, his mother fell asleep. The knowledge was cut off.

Abhimanyu enters the formation with extraordinary skill, fighting valiantly. He can see exactly what is happening. His understanding is complete. But he has no way out. He is destroyed not by ignorance but by structural incapacity.

The Abhimanyu Conundrum is this exact configuration. High context awareness, blocked response availability. The system sees the problem with perfect clarity — and has no mechanism to act on what it sees.

What it looks like
The Abhimanyu Pattern
In organisations Leadership team that accurately diagnoses the strategic problem but cannot mobilise the organisation to execute. The insight exists. The bureaucracy, politics, or resource constraints block the response.
In behaviour change The person who understands exactly what they need to do, why they need to do it, and what's in the way — but lives in an environment that makes the required behaviour structurally impossible.
In policy A government that has identified the right intervention but lacks enforcement capacity, delivery infrastructure, or institutional alignment to implement it.
→ Prescription: Restore A. Do not add more awareness.
08 / 15
The Myth of Hanuman

He forgot
his own power.
Until someone reminded him.

In the Ramayana, Hanuman — the divine warrior — was cursed as a child to forget his extraordinary abilities. He lived as an ordinary being, unaware of what he was capable of. Only when the sage Jambavan reminded him of his true nature did Hanuman remember — and then he leapt across the ocean to Lanka in a single bound.

The problem was never capacity. Hanuman always had the power. The problem was the absence of an accurate model of what he was and what was possible.

The Hanuman Complex is the dangerous inversion. High response availability, degraded context awareness. The system has capacity — sometimes enormous capacity — but no accurate model to direct it. It acts. But it acts blind.

What it looks like
The Hanuman Pattern
In organisations A well-resourced team launching initiative after initiative without a clear model of what the underlying problem actually is. High activity, low traction. Each effort generates sunk cost and political fatigue without coherent direction.
In behaviour change The highly motivated, well-supported person who keeps trying — new programmes, new approaches, new commitments — without an accurate model of what is actually driving their behaviour. Each failure depletes resources and self-efficacy.
In policy A delivery-capable government implementing programmes without adequate diagnostic grounding. Strong execution of the wrong intervention. The capacity to act fast makes the misallocation worse.
→ Prescription: Stop adding A. Restore I first. Hard stop.

The Asymmetry

Abhimanyu is the better failure.
Hanuman is the dangerous one.

This is not intuitive. Organisations and practitioners systematically prefer high-capacity, high-activity states — and are suspicious of the quiet, aware-but-blocked configuration.

But Abhimanyu preserves optionality. The model is intact. When capacity becomes available — through intervention, structural change, or resource provision — the system can act correctly immediately, because it already knows what to do.

Hanuman does not have this property. Restoring capacity to a system without a valid model just gives it a faster machine pointed in the wrong direction. Each action takes the system further from the right answer and makes it harder to change course — committed errors accumulate.

Abhimanyu — I+, A−
Crisis is deeper when it arrives. But recovery is faster. The model is still pointing correctly. One intervention — restoring A — is sufficient.
Hanuman — I−, A+
Crisis is shallower individually. But structurally persistent. Each action compounds the misallocation. Recovery requires two steps: stop the current trajectory, then rebuild the model.
The Critical Rule
Adding capacity to a Hanuman system before restoring its model is the most common and most costly intervention error.

The Sequencing Law

State determines
sequence. Sequence
determines outcome.

The central result of CARA is a sequencing law. Before selecting an intervention, you must know which of the two functions is the binding constraint — and address it first. Order is not a preference. It is structurally determined.

The right intervention applied in the wrong order does not just underperform. It converts a recoverable state into a more dangerous one.
01

Trooper State I− A−

Build context awareness first. Always. Do not provide resources, action plans, or execution support before the system has an accurate model. Premature capacity becomes Hanuman Complex.
02

Abhimanyu Conundrum I+ A−

Restore response availability only. Do not add more awareness — the model is intact. Target the structural, environmental, or institutional constraints on execution.
03

Hanuman Complex I− A+

Stop adding capacity. Hard stop. Rebuild the model first. Only once context awareness is restored does additional response availability become productive.
04

Arjuna State I+ A+

Monitor for drift. The leading warning is context awareness degrading before response availability. Watch I, not A — I erodes first.

Beyond Snapshot — Trajectory

Where a system is
matters less than
where it is going.

State classification gives you position. Trajectory gives you momentum. Six recurring dynamic patterns emerge from how I and A are moving — each with a distinct risk profile and a distinct intervention logic.

Pattern Signature What it means Intervention
Bubble
Precursor
 I stable, A falling from positive System in false alignment. A quietly eroding while I holds. The warning arrives before the state changes. Watch. Do not prop A artificially.
Late Cycle
 I high, A just crossed zero The internal break has happened. I still elevated. System at highest near-term risk. Urgent. Force model update first.
Trapped
Frustration
 I high, A negative and worsening Model intact, execution collapsing with no floor. Highest forward risk of all patterns. Emergency A-restoration only.
Freefall
 Both I and A collapsing Active breakdown. No leading variable. Triage mode. Minimum viable A to stop bleeding. Then I.
Blind
Acceleration
 I negative, A rising Capacity growing without a valid model. Committed errors accumulating with each action. Hard stop on A. Rebuild I completely first.
Recovery
Signal
 I turning up, A still low I-led recovery underway. Correct sequence. Fragile — do not rush. Protect the I recovery. Let A follow naturally.

Application — Organisational Change

Why transformation
programmes stall.

Case Study
The Restructuring That Generated More Problems

A mid-size professional services firm launches a strategic transformation. New operating model, new incentive structures, new technology platform. Board-level support, adequate budget, experienced consultants. By every resource measure, the conditions are right.

The CARA Diagnosis
The leadership team had an accurate model of the problem (client model was outdated, margins declining). But the middle layer — team leads, practice heads — had no accurate model of why the changes were necessary or what the new model required of them.

I was high at the top. I was low where execution lived. The organisation was simultaneously Abhimanyu (top) and Trooper (middle).
What Was Applied
Significant A-restoration: restructuring, new systems, new processes, new accountabilities. Cascaded fast.

This is the classic Hanuman error applied at scale. Response availability was forced into a layer of the organisation whose context awareness had not been built. The new systems were used to execute the old mental models. Committed errors accumulated.
The CARA Prescription
Delay A-restoration by one cycle. Run an intensive I-building phase with middle leadership first — not information cascade, but genuine model-building: what are we actually trying to do, why does this structure enable it, what does success look like in your role?

Only once I is stable at the execution layer does A-restoration produce alignment rather than faster misallocation.

Application — Behaviour Change & Policy

The two most common
intervention errors.

Error Type 1 — Behaviour Change
Action planning for Trooper-state clients

The most common error in behaviour change practice. A person enters support in Trooper state — low accurate self-model of what drives their behaviour, constrained environment. The practitioner, using COM-B or Fogg, correctly identifies low capability and low opportunity.

The standard response is an action plan, implementation intentions, and resource provision. This is A-restoration for a system without adequate I.

The action plan gets used to execute the person's existing — inaccurate — model of their situation. It produces activity that looks like engagement and produces no change.

→ CARA: Build I first. Motivational Interviewing is an I-restoration tool. Deploy it fully before any action planning.
Error Type 2 — Development Policy
Awareness campaigns for Abhimanyu communities

A second common error, running in the opposite direction. A community — or population — has an accurate model of their situation. They understand the health risk, the economic constraint, the structural injustice. They are in Abhimanyu state.

The intervention is an awareness campaign, a psychoeducation programme, an information resource. More I-building for a system where I is not the constraint.

The community knows about the food desert, the underfunded school, the inaccessible healthcare. Telling them about it again — however elegantly — changes nothing.

→ CARA: They are Abhimanyu. The constraint is A. Build delivery, access, structural opportunity — not awareness.

The CARA Protocol

CARA as a diagnostic
prior to intervention.

CARA does not replace COM-B, Fogg, or any domain framework. It runs before them. It tells you which section of the tool library to open — and which sections will make things worse.

Step 01

Assess I

How accurate is the system's current model of its situation? Can it correctly describe the drivers of its behaviour and anticipate consequences?

Step 02

Assess A

What structural constraints and enablers actually exist? What is the system's real degree of freedom to act — not self-reported confidence, but environmental reality?

Step 03

Classify State

From the signs of I and A, assign one of the four states. This is the diagnostic output — not an intervention recommendation yet.

Step 04

Read Trajectory

Is I improving, stable, or falling? Is A improving, stable, or falling? Which direction is the gap moving? This tells you urgency and sequence.

Step 05

Then Intervene

Now reach for the domain tool library. CARA tells you which category — I-building, A-restoration, maintenance, or triage. The specific technique is domain knowledge.

Monitor
After any intervention, monitor I first. Context awareness degrades before response availability in every domain studied. Behaviour change is a lagging signal. Model drift is the early warning.

The Theory

A general theory of
intervention sequencing.

CARA is not a replacement for domain expertise. It is the question that must come before domain expertise is applied. What state is the system in? Which function is the binding constraint? What direction is each moving?

The framework has been validated across macroeconomic systems, political institutions, immune response, governance, education, and individual behaviour — consistently showing that state and trajectory determine intervention responsiveness, and that misidentifying state converts well-resourced interventions into committed errors.

It does not tell systems what to do.
It tells them how not to fail while trying.
